Who founded it???

The beginnings of the Church of England, from which The Episcopal Church derives, date to at least the second century, when merchants and other travelers first brought Christianity to England. It is customary to regard St. Augustine of Canterbury's mission to England in 597 as marking the formal beginning of the church under papal authority, as it was to be throughout the Middle Ages. In its modern form, the church dates from the English Reformation of the 16th century, when royal supremacy was established and the authority of the papacy was repudiated. With the advent of British colonization, the Church of England was established on every continent. In time, these churches gained their independence, but retained connections with the mother church in the Anglican Communion.

Important Dates

1529-36: Henry VIII and Parliament take over the administration of the Church in England (link is external). Destruction of monasteries ensues.
1611: King James Version of the Bible (link is external) is published.
1785: The First General Convention of Episcopal Church is held, with clergy and lay representatives from Delaware, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, and Virginia. The General Convention authorizes the preparation of an American Prayer Book and names itself the Protestant Episcopal Church in the United States of America.
1786: The proposed American Book of Common Prayer (link is external) is approved for use on a state-by-state basis.
1804: Absalom Jones is ordained the first black priest in the Episcopal Church.

The Episcopal Church and America

The ideology of the Episcopalian Church doesn't differ greatly from Roman Catholicism. In essence, the episcopalian faith is just an offshoot of Catholicism. The founders of America used the episcopal faith as a form of rebellion against the Church of England. Instead of aligning with Henry VIII and the Church of England, American colonists, hell bent on not following the old ways, founded a new denomination. Christ Church was the first symbol of the Episcopal Church in America. Known as “The Nation’s Church”, Christ Church welcomed well known people like first president George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, Betsy Ross and 15 signers of the Declaration of Independence. Christ Church was founded in 1695 as a part William Penn’s “Holy Experiment” which, coupled with the Charter of Privileges, allowed all denominations freedom of worship in the New Land. The congregation of the church quickly became the largest in all of Philadelphia.
